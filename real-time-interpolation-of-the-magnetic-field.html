Real time interpolation of the magnetic field

Wed 10 May 2017
By Simon
Category: algorithms

This report introduces one of my attempts at modelling the magnetic field of a room using a continuous representation. The first goal of this work is to assist the tracking of the orientation of a device by taking into account the multiple distortions of the field observed indoor: this is a step toward a positioning algorithm that takes advantage of these anomalies.

Maxwell's equations and the magnetic scalar potential

In order to be plausible, an approximation of the magnetic field should obey some properties well known by physicists. All electromagnetic phenomena are governed by Maxwell's equations, two of them involve the magnetic field:

∇⋅B=0 \nabla \cdot B = 0 ∇⋅B=0

∇×B=μ0(J+ϵ0∂E∂t) \nabla \times B = \mu_0 \left(J + \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}\right) ∇×B=μ0​(J+ϵ0​∂t∂E​)

where B∈R3↦R3B \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mapsto \mathbb{R}^3B∈R3↦R3 is the magnetic field, μ0\mu_0μ0​ and ϵ0\epsilon_0ϵ0​ are respectively the permeability and permittivity of vacuum, JJJ is the current density and EEE the electric field.

The first of these two laws states that the magnetic field is divergence free, in other words, magnetic monopoles do not exist. We are assuming that there is no free current in the air and no time-dependent effects due to moving electric charges, thus the Ampère's circuital law reduces to:

∇×B=0 \nabla \times B = 0 ∇×B=0

In these conditions, the magnetic field is then said to be irrotational. An irrotational vector field can be fully described as the gradient of a scalar field, called a scalar potential:

B=−∇ψ B = - \nabla \psi B=−∇ψ

Thus, by modeling ψ\psiψ in place of BBB directly, we are implicitly modeling a vector field that follows the second law.12 The first law will be added as a regularization in the optimization procedure.

Differentiable interpolation

The map is represented by a set of anchor points M={(ck,wk)∣k∈[1,K]}M = \{(c_k, w_k) | k \in [1, K]\}M={(ck​,wk​)∣k∈[1,K]}, where ckc_kck​ is the position of the anchor in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 and wkw_kwk​ its associated value – a scalar potential in this case.

In order to have a model differentiable with respect to the position and the elements of the map MMM, the interpolation function is defined to be in the form:

ψ(x)=∑k=1Kwkϕ(x,ck) \psi(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \phi(x, c_k) ψ(x)=k=1∑K​wk​ϕ(x,ck​)

where x∈R2x \in \mathbb{R}^2x∈R2 is a point in space, wkw_kwk​ the value of the map at the anchor kkk located in position ckc_kck​, and ϕ∈(R2,R2)↦R\phi \in (\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R}^2) \mapsto \mathbb{R}ϕ∈(R2,R2)↦R is a differentiable radial basis function: it gives a weight to the anchors depending on their distance to the point xxx. In essence, this approach is similar to the attention mechanism in deep learning.3

With this definition of the scalar potential function ψ\psiψ, the estimated magnetic field BBB is:

B(x)=∇xψ(x)=∑k=1Kwk∇xϕ(x,ck) B(x) = \nabla_x \psi(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \nabla_x \phi(x, c_k) B(x)=∇x​ψ(x)=k=1∑K​wk​∇x​ϕ(x,ck​)

We dropped the negative sign to simplify the equations even though this not standard among mathematicians and physicists, however the true value of the potential is of little interest to our application.

Learning the map parameters

Given a set of known points S⋆={(xi⋆,yi⋆)∣i∈[1..N]}S^\star = \{ (x_i^\star, y_i^\star) | i \in [1..N] \}S⋆={(xi⋆​,yi⋆​)∣i∈[1..N]} where xi⋆∈R2x_i^\star \in \mathbb{R}^2xi⋆​∈R2 is a position in space and yi⋆∈R2y_i^\star \in \mathbb{R}^2yi⋆​∈R2 the observed value of the BBB field at this position, we are looking for a differentiable function ψ∈R2↦R\psi \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mapsto \mathbb{R}ψ∈R2↦R that minimizes the loss

Lψ=∑i=1Nδ(∇ψ(xi⋆),yi⋆) \mathcal{L}_\psi = \sum_{i=1}^N \delta(\nabla \psi(x_i^\star), y_i^\star) Lψ​=i=1∑N​δ(∇ψ(xi⋆​),yi⋆​)

where δ∈(R2,R2)↦R\delta \in (\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R}^2) \mapsto \mathbb{R}δ∈(R2,R2)↦R is a measurement of the error between the output of the model and the expected value.

Stochastic gradient descent

Considering the parameters as θ=(w1,⋯ ,wK,c1,⋯ ,cK)⊤\theta = (w_1, \cdots, w_K, c_1, \cdots, c_K)^\topθ=(w1​,⋯,wK​,c1​,⋯,cK​)⊤. Minimizing the loss Lψ\mathcal{L}_\psiLψ​ using a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) comes down to updating the parameters iteratively using

θ←θ−ϵ∇θδ(∇xψ(x⋆),y⋆) \theta \leftarrow \theta - \epsilon \nabla_\theta \delta(\nabla_x \psi(x^\star), y^\star) θ←θ−ϵ∇θ​δ(∇x​ψ(x⋆),y⋆)

where ϵ\epsilonϵ is a constant controlling the learning rate and (x⋆,y⋆)(x^\star, y^\star)(x⋆,y⋆) is an element of S⋆S^\starS⋆ chosen at random. This is the simplest form of SGD, in practice there is many improvements to make it much more effective.

Implementation using a Kalman filter

We chose ϕ\phiϕ to be the form of a Gaussian function such that

ϕ(x,ck)=e−∣∣x−ck∣∣22σ2 \phi(x, c_k) = e^{-\frac{||x - c_k||^2}{2 \sigma^2}} ϕ(x,ck​)=e−2σ2∣∣x−ck​∣∣2​

where σ\sigmaσ is a hyperparameter related to the full width at half maximum – it gives the spread of the weighting. Its gradient w.r.t the position xxx is

∇xϕ(x,ck)=ck−xσ2e−∣∣x−ck∣∣22σ2 \nabla_x \phi(x, c_k) = \frac{c_k - x}{\sigma^2} e^{-\frac{||x - c_k||^2}{2 \sigma^2}} ∇x​ϕ(x,ck​)=σ2ck​−x​e−2σ2∣∣x−ck​∣∣2​

Therefore, we have

B(x)=∑k=1Kwk∇xϕ(x,ck)=∑k=1Kwkck−xσ2e−∣∣x−ck∣∣22σ2 B(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \nabla_x \phi(x, c_k) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \frac{c_k - x}{\sigma^2} e^{-\frac{||x - c_k||^2}{2 \sigma^2}} B(x)=k=1∑K​wk​∇x​ϕ(x,ck​)=k=1∑K​wk​σ2ck​−x​e−2σ2∣∣x−ck​∣∣2​

By fixing the values of the ckc_kck​ – i.e. taking them as hyperparameters of the model – we can now express B(x)B(x)B(x) as a linear function of the parameters we are trying to estimate (the weights wkw_kwk​) such that:

B=H⋅w B = H \cdot w B=H⋅w

where HHH is the 3×K3 \times K3×K matrix

H=[∇xϕ(x,c1)⋯∇xϕ(x,cK)] H = \begin{bmatrix} \nabla_x \phi(x, c_1) & \cdots & \nabla_x \phi(x, c_K) \end{bmatrix} H=[∇x​ϕ(x,c1​)​⋯​∇x​ϕ(x,cK​)​]

and www the column vector

w=[w1⋮wK] w = \begin{bmatrix} w_1 \\ \vdots \\ w_K \end{bmatrix} w=⎣⎢⎢⎡​w1​⋮wK​​⎦⎥⎥⎤​

When defined as a linear optimization problem, the quest for the most likely field potential fits in the (original) Kalman filter framework – which is, under some conditions, a well suited algorithm for real time estimation of unknown variables. In particular, we are making use of the update step of the algorithm during which observations are used to correct the estimation of the state of the system.

An update iteration of the estimated state www and its covariance PPP given a measurement yyy goes as follow:

  • Compute the HHH matrix for the current position xxx
  • Compute the residual zzz between the true and estimated measurements, its covariance SSS and the Kalman gain KKK using

z=y−HwS=R+HPH⊤K=PH⊤S−1 \begin{aligned} z &= y - H w \\ S &= R + H P H^\top \\ K &= P H^\top S^{-1} \\ \end{aligned} zSK​=y−Hw=R+HPH⊤=PH⊤S−1​

  • Update the state www and its covariance PPP with

w←w+KzP←(I−KH)P \begin{aligned} w &\leftarrow w + K z \\ P &\leftarrow (I - K H) P \end{aligned} wP​←w+Kz←(I−KH)P​

In our case, the measurement yyy is the output of the magnetometer in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 (transformed to take into account the orientation of the device). Thus, SSS is in M3×3\mathcal{M}^{3\times3}M3×3 and its inverse is easy to compute.

Initial results

In the following experiment, we choose the ckc_kck​ such that the points are spread over a grid covering a least the area of interest with a resolution of 25cm. The model observes 60 samples taken at random from a ground truth made of 380 samples. In other words, the train set is made of N=60N = 60N=60 samples.

Future work

Regularization using the divergence

We used only one property of the magnetic field, the divergence-free property could be introduced in the system as a regularizer such that:

∇⋅B(x)=∇⋅∇ψ(x)=∑k=1Kwk∇x⋅∇xϕ(x,ck) \nabla \cdot B(x) = \nabla \cdot \nabla \psi(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \nabla_x \cdot \nabla_x \phi(x, c_k) ∇⋅B(x)=∇⋅∇ψ(x)=k=1∑K​wk​∇x​⋅∇x​ϕ(x,ck​)

which can be rewritten using the scalar Laplacian:

∇⋅B(x)=∑k=1KwkΔxϕ(x,ck) \nabla \cdot B(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K w_k \Delta_x \phi(x, c_k) ∇⋅B(x)=k=1∑K​wk​Δx​ϕ(x,ck​)

In a Kalman filter framework, this would come down to making an observation of the value of the divergence.
